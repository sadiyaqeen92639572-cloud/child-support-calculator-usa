How Michigan Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Determine each parent's net income (gross income minus taxes, mandatory retirement, and other allowable deductions).
  2. If a parent's net income is at or below the Low Income Threshold ($1,255/month), that parent's base support obligation is simply 10% of their income.
  3. Otherwise, combine both parents' net incomes into family income, look up the General Care Support Table bracket for that family income and number of children, and apply the General Care Equation: Base Support + (Marginal Percentage × amount over the bracket threshold), multiplied by that parent's income share.
  4. Apply the Parental Time Offset Equation using each parent's approximate annual overnights, which produces the final monthly transfer amount directly — a negative result means Parent A pays, positive means Parent B pays.

Michigan Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: Parent A net income $4,500/month (60% share), Parent B net income $3,000/month (40% share), 2 children, Parent A has 100 overnights/year, Parent B has 265.

  1. Family income: $7,500/month, bracket: $7,198.27 (2 children) — Base $2,095.42 + 20.20% × ($7,500−$7,198.27) = $2,156.32.
  2. Parent A's base obligation: $2,156.32 × 60% = $1,293.79.
  3. Parent B's base obligation: $2,156.32 × 40% = $862.53.
  4. Parental Time Offset: (100^2.5 × 862.53 − 265^2.5 × 1293.79) / (100^2.5 + 265^2.5) — a negative result, so Parent A pays the guideline amount.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in Michigan?

Determine each parent's net income (gross income minus taxes, mandatory retirement, and other allowable deductions). If a parent's net income is at or below the Low Income Threshold ($1,255/month), that parent's base support obligation is simply 10% of their income. Otherwise, combine both parents' net incomes into family income, look up the General Care Support Table bracket for that family income and number of children, and apply the General Care Equation: Base Support + (Marginal Percentage × amount over the bracket threshold), multiplied by that parent's income share. Apply the Parental Time Offset Equation using each parent's approximate annual overnights, which produces the final monthly transfer amount directly — a negative result means Parent A pays, positive means Parent B pays.

How does Michigan's Parental Time Offset work?

Michigan's Parental Time Offset Equation takes each parent's base support obligation (from the General Care Support Table) and each parent's approximate annual overnights, and computes a single net transfer amount directly — it isn't a simple percentage credit. A negative result means Parent A pays; a positive result means Parent B pays. More overnights for the paying parent reduces what they owe, but through this specific equation rather than a flat percentage table.

What is Michigan's Low Income Threshold?

Michigan's Low Income Threshold is $1,255/month in net income (2025 MCSF). A parent at or below that threshold has their base support obligation set at simply 10% of their income, instead of being run through the General Care Support Table — a simplified calculation meant to avoid pushing very low earners below subsistence.

What counts as net income in Michigan?

Net income under the Michigan Child Support Formula is gross income minus taxes, mandatory retirement contributions, and other allowable deductions specifically defined in the MCSF Manual — not just whatever shows up as take-home pay on a paystub, since some deductions employers apply (like voluntary retirement or optional benefits) aren't allowed reductions under the formula.
