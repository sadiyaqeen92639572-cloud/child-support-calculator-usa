How North Carolina Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Determine each parent's monthly adjusted gross income, then combine both for total combined adjusted gross income.
  2. If the paying parent's own adjusted gross income is below $1,150/month, a flat $50/month minimum order applies (absent deviation).
  3. If the paying parent's income is above $1,150/month but within the self-support reserve "shaded" zone for the number of children ($1,750 for 1 child up to $3,450 for 6 children), the obligation is looked up using the paying parent's own income alone — not combined income — with no childcare or health insurance add-ons.
  4. Otherwise, look up the basic child support obligation from the official Schedule of Basic Support Obligations using combined adjusted gross income and number of children.
  5. Each parent's share equals their income divided by combined income, applied to the basic obligation plus reasonable work-related child care costs and health insurance premiums.
  6. This models Worksheet A only (one parent has primary physical custody, defined as 243+ nights/year). Worksheet B (shared custody, both parents 123+ nights/year) and Worksheet C (split custody) use different calculations not modeled here.
  7. Above $40,000/month combined adjusted gross income, courts determine support case-by-case rather than extending the schedule.

What is North Carolina's self-support reserve, and how does it affect low-income parents?

North Carolina builds a self-support reserve into its child support schedule, based on the 2022 federal poverty level for one person ($1,133/month). If the paying parent's own adjusted gross income is below $1,150/month, a flat $50/month minimum order applies absent a deviation. Between $1,150/month and a higher ceiling that varies by number of children ($1,750 for one child up to $3,450 for six children), the obligation is calculated from the paying parent's income alone rather than combined income, and childcare/health insurance costs are not added on top — a mechanic most online calculators skip entirely.

What is the difference between Worksheet A, B, and C in North Carolina?

Worksheet A applies when one parent (or a third party) has primary physical custody of all the children — defined as 243+ nights per year. Worksheet B applies when parents share custody (each has the child at least 123 nights per year) of all children, or share custody of some while one has primary custody of others; the combined basic obligation is increased 50% before being prorated. Worksheet C applies when primary custody of two or more children is split between the parents. This calculator models Worksheet A only.

What happens if combined income is above $40,000/month in North Carolina?

Above $40,000/month in combined adjusted gross income, North Carolina's official schedule has no table value. Courts set support to meet the child's reasonable needs for health, education, and maintenance under G.S. 50-13.4(c), using the schedule only as a possible reference point for a minimal level of support.
