How Nevada Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Nevada is NOT an income-shares state -- the base obligation is a tiered percentage of the OBLIGOR's own gross monthly income only, never combined income.
  2. For the first $6,000/month: 16% for one child, 22% for two, 26% for three, 28% for four, plus 2% for each additional child.
  3. For the portion between $6,000 and $10,000/month: 8% for one child, 11% for two, 13% for three, 14% for four, plus 1% for each additional child.
  4. For the portion above $10,000/month: 4% for one child, 6% for two, 6% for three, 7% for four, plus 0.5% for each additional child -- there is no income cap.
  5. The obligor (paying parent) is determined by which parent has the minority of overnights; Nevada has no mandatory shared-placement cross-formula -- courts adjust for time-sharing at their discretion under NAC 425.150.
  6. If the obligor's income is very low, courts may use a separately published low-income schedule (NAC 425.145, updated annually by the Administrative Office of the Courts) instead -- not modeled by this calculator.

Nevada Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example (the regulation's own worked example): obligor earns $14,000/month, 2 children.

  1. First $6,000: $6,000 × 22% = $1,320.
  2. Portion between $6,000-$10,000 ($4,000): $4,000 × 11% = $440.
  3. Portion above $10,000 ($4,000): $4,000 × 6% = $240.
  4. Total base child support obligation: $1,320 + $440 + $240 = $2,000/month.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in Nevada?

Nevada is NOT an income-shares state -- the base obligation is a tiered percentage of the OBLIGOR's own gross monthly income only, never combined income. Is Nevada an income-shares state?

No. Nevada's Base Child Support Obligation (NAC 425.140) is based entirely on the OBLIGOR's (paying parent's) own gross monthly income -- the other parent's income doesn't factor into the base calculation at all, unlike income-shares states such as Texas or Georgia.

Does Nevada have an income cap for child support?

No. Unlike most states, Nevada's formula has no upper income limit -- the top tier (income above $10,000/month) simply continues to apply its percentage (4%-8% depending on number of children) to all income above that threshold, however high.

Does shared custody reduce child support in Nevada?

Not automatically through a fixed formula. Nevada courts may adjust the base obligation under NAC 425.150 considering the relative income of both households and time-sharing costs, among other discretionary factors -- but there is no mandatory shared-placement cross-formula like some other states use.
