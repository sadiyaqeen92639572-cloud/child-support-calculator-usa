How Alaska Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Alaska is a flat-percentage-of-income state (not income shares): the child support award is a percentage of the NON-CUSTODIAL parent's own adjusted annual income only -- the custodial parent's income is not part of the base calculation.
  2. "Adjusted annual income" starts from total income and subtracts mandatory deductions (income tax, Social Security/self-employment tax, Medicare tax, mandatory union dues and retirement contributions), work-related child care costs for the children, health/life insurance premiums (within limits), and support already owed for other children.
  3. Multiply adjusted annual income (capped at $138,000/year, or $11,500/month) by the percentage for the number of children: 20% for one child, 27% for two, 33% for three, plus an extra 3% for each additional child.
  4. The minimum child support award is $50/month, regardless of income.
  5. This is Rule 90.3(a)'s primary-custody calculation only; shared, divided, or hybrid custody (Rule 90.3(b)) use a materially different formula involving both parents' figures and are not modeled here.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in Alaska?

Alaska is a flat-percentage-of-income state (not income shares): the child support award is a percentage of the NON-CUSTODIAL parent's own adjusted annual income only -- the custodial parent's income is not part of the base calculation. "Adjusted annual income" starts from total income and subtracts mandatory deductions (income tax, Social Security/self-employment tax, Medicare tax, mandatory union dues and retirement contributions), work-related child care costs for the children, health/life insurance premiums (within limits), and support already owed for other children. Multiply adjusted annual income (capped at $138,000/year, or $11,500/month) by the percentage for the number of children: 20% for one child, 27% for two, 33% for three, plus an extra 3% for each additional child. The minimum child support award is $50/month, regardless of income. This is Rule 90.3(a)'s primary-custody calculation only; shared, divided, or hybrid custody (Rule 90.3(b)) use a materially different formula involving both parents' figures and are not modeled here.

Does Alaska use combined parental income like most states?

No. Alaska Civil Rule 90.3(a) calculates the award as a flat percentage of the NON-CUSTODIAL parent's own adjusted income only -- the custodial parent's income does not factor into the primary-custody calculation, unlike income-shares states such as Texas or Georgia.

What counts as a parent's "adjusted annual income" in Alaska?

Total income from all sources, minus mandatory deductions (taxes, Social Security, Medicare, mandatory retirement contributions), work-related child care costs, certain health and life insurance premiums, and support already paid for other children (Rule 90.3(a)(1)).

Is there a different rule for low-income parents in Alaska?

Yes. Under Rule 90.3(a)(5), a parent with total annual income of $30,000 or less may use the LESSER of the standard itemized-deduction calculation or their total income minus $7,500/year as their adjusted income -- often producing a lower support amount. This calculator does not model that alternate calculation; consult Alaska's official child support calculator or a family law attorney if this may apply to you.

Methodology & Source

Formula model: percentage of income. Effective 2023-10-16, guideline version 2023-AK-v1, last reviewed 2026-07-13.

Official source: Alaska Court System — Alaska Civil Rule 90.3 (Alaska Civil Rule 90.3(a)).

This calculator models Alaska Civil Rule 90.3(a), the PRIMARY PHYSICAL CUSTODY calculation only, applied to a single non-custodial parent's already-adjusted net income. It does not model: the Rule 90.3(a)(5) Low-Income Adjustment (parents with total annual income of $30,000 or less may use total income minus $7,500/yr instead of the standard itemized-deduction adjusted income, whichever is lower -- this requires a gross-income-and-deductions breakdown beyond this calculator's single net-income input); the Rule 90.3(a)(3) Extended Visitation Credit (discretionary, up to 75% reduction for periods of 27+ consecutive days); Shared, Divided, or Hybrid Physical Custody (Rule 90.3(b), an entirely different calculation requiring both parents' figures and a 1.5x multiplier); or the grandparent-support provision (Rule 90.3(c)(4)). Courts may also vary the award for good cause under Rule 90.3(c)(1).
