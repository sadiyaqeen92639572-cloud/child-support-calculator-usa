How Hawaii Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Hawaii uses a Modified Melson Formula -- one of only 3 states (with Delaware and Montana) that use this model, though Hawaii's version differs from Delaware's in several ways.
  2. Net Income for each parent is gross income minus taxes (2022 federal/state/FICA tables, single filer) and the Self-Support Reserve ($1,693/month, 130% of the one-person federal poverty guideline).
  3. The Primary Child Support Need is a flat $455 per child (no separate per-household allowance, unlike Delaware), plus childcare and health insurance costs, prorated by each parent's share of combined Net Income.
  4. SOLA Income for each parent is gross income minus $1,303 (a separate, smaller deduction than the Self-Support Reserve). The combined SOLA Income minus the Primary Need is the pool shared with the children, at a flat 10% per child, capped at 30% (unlike Delaware's tiered 12/17/21%).
  5. The paying parent's obligation generally may not exceed their own Net Income, and a minimum of $91/child/month applies.
  6. Extensive Time-Sharing and Equal Time-Sharing use separate worksheets with different calculations, not modeled here.

Hawaii Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: Parent A earns $2,500/mo, Parent B earns $2,000/mo, 3 children, Parent A is the noncustodial parent.

  1. Net Income (this calculator's approximation, Self-Support Reserve only): Parent A = $2,500 - $1,693 = $807. Parent B = $2,000 - $1,693 = $307. Combined = $1,114.
  2. Primary Need: 3 × $455 = $1,365. Parent A's share (807/1,114 = 72.4%): ≈$989 -- but capped at Parent A's own Net Income of $807.
  3. SOLA Income: Parent A = $2,500-$1,303=$1,197. Parent B = $2,000-$1,303=$697. Combined $1,894, remaining after Primary Need ($1,365) = $529. SOLA % for 3 children = 30% (capped): $159 SOLA amount.
  4. Parent A's total theoretical obligation exceeds their own Net Income, so it caps at $807/month.

Formula (Hawaii's Modified Melson Formula)

Net Income(parent) = max(0, gross_income(parent) - Self-Support Reserve)
share(parent) = Net Income(parent) / combined Net Income
Primary Need = children × Base Primary Support + childcare + health insurance
SOLA Income(parent) = max(0, gross_income(parent) - SOLA income deduction)
Remaining SOLA = max(0, combined SOLA Income - Primary Need)
SOLA Amount = Remaining SOLA × min(30%, 10% × children)
support = min(paying parent's Net Income, paying parent's share × (Primary Need + SOLA Amount))

FAQ

How is child support calculated in Hawaii?

Hawaii uses a Modified Melson Formula -- one of only 3 states (with Delaware and Montana) that use this model, though Hawaii's version differs from Delaware's in several ways. Net Income for each parent is gross income minus taxes (2022 federal/state/FICA tables, single filer) and the Self-Support Reserve ($1,693/month, 130% of the one-person federal poverty guideline). The Primary Child Support Need is a flat $455 per child (no separate per-household allowance, unlike Delaware), plus childcare and health insurance costs, prorated by each parent's share of combined Net Income. SOLA Income for each parent is gross income minus $1,303 (a separate, smaller deduction than the Self-Support Reserve). The combined SOLA Income minus the Primary Need is the pool shared with the children, at a flat 10% per child, capped at 30% (unlike Delaware's tiered 12/17/21%). The paying parent's obligation generally may not exceed their own Net Income, and a minimum of $91/child/month applies. Extensive Time-Sharing and Equal Time-Sharing use separate worksheets with different calculations, not modeled here.

How is Hawaii's Melson Formula different from Delaware's?

Both use the same 3-step Melson concept (self-support first, then primary need, then a Standard of Living Adjustment), but the specific numbers differ: Hawaii's Base Primary Support is a flat $455/child with no separate per-household allowance, while Delaware adds a per-household component. Hawaii's SOLA is a flat 10% per child capped at 30%, while Delaware uses tiered percentages (12%/17%/21%) plus a High Income Offset that Hawaii doesn't have.

What is Hawaii's Self-Support Reserve?

The Self-Support Reserve is $1,693/month (130% of the one-person federal poverty guideline), subtracted from a parent's income before calculating their Net Income for child support purposes. Hawaii's official calculation also subtracts actual income taxes using 2022 tax tables -- this calculator approximates Net Income using only the Self-Support Reserve deduction, so its result may run somewhat higher than an official calculation.

Is there a minimum or maximum child support amount in Hawaii?

The minimum is $91 per child per month. The paying parent's obligation generally should not exceed their own monthly Net Income, if that Net Income exceeds $91 per child -- a cap built directly into the calculation, not left to judicial discretion.
