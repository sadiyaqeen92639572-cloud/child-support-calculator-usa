How Delaware Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Delaware uses the Melson Formula -- one of only 3 states (with Hawaii and Montana) that use this three-step model instead of income shares or a flat percentage.
  2. Step 1: Each parent keeps a Self-Support Allowance ($1,600/month as of 2026, 120% of the one-person federal poverty guideline) before anything else is counted -- this comes off gross income first.
  3. Step 2: The children's Primary Support need (a per-child allowance of $420 plus a per-household allowance of $380, plus childcare and health insurance) is prorated between the parents by their share of remaining income (Net Available Income, NAI).
  4. Step 3: The Standard of Living Adjustment (SOLA) shares each parent's LEFTOVER income (after their own primary share) with the children, at a percentage that rises with the number of children (12% for one, 17% for two, 21% for three, +2% each additional) -- simulating what the children would enjoy if the family lived together.
  5. A High Income Offset reduces the SOLA base by 30% of any combined income above 10 times the Self-Support Allowance ($16,000/month combined).
  6. A parenting-time credit (10% at 80-124 overnights/year, 30% at 125-163) reduces the paying parent's obligation, and a Self-Support Protection caps the obligation at 50% of that parent's own Net Available Income.
  7. A minimum order applies: $160/month for one child, $240/month for two or more.

Delaware Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: Parent A earns $5,000/mo, Parent B earns $3,000/mo, 2 children, Parent B is the noncustodial parent with 0 overnights.

  1. Net Available Income: Parent A = $5,000 - $1,600 = $3,400. Parent B = $3,000 - $1,600 = $1,400. Combined = $4,800.
  2. Primary Support Need: 2 × $420 + $380 = $1,220. Parent B's share (1,400/4,800 = 29.2%): $356.
  3. Parent B's NAI for SOLA: $1,400 - $356 = $1,044. Combined NAI for SOLA ($3,580) × 17% SOLA rate = $609 total, Parent B's 29.2% share ≈ $178.
  4. Parent B's total obligation: $356 (primary) + $178 (SOLA) ≈ $533/month.

Primary Obligation(parent) = share(parent) × Primary Need

FAQ

What is the Melson Formula, and why does only Delaware use it (with Hawaii and Montana)?

The Melson Formula is a three-step child support model, named after a Delaware Family Court judge, that first guarantees each parent a Self-Support Allowance before anything else counts, then prorates the children's basic Primary Support need, and finally shares each parent's LEFTOVER income with the children through a Standard of Living Adjustment (SOLA). Only Delaware, Hawaii, and Montana use this model -- most other states use income shares (combining both parents' income and looking up a table) or a flat percentage of the paying parent's income.

What is Delaware's Standard of Living Adjustment (SOLA)?

After each parent's Self-Support Allowance and share of the children's Primary Support need are set aside, the SOLA shares a percentage of what's LEFT OVER with the children -- 12% for one child, 17% for two, 21% for three, plus 2% for each additional child. This simulates the standard of living children would have enjoyed if their parents lived together, rather than just covering basic needs. A High Income Offset reduces this at very high incomes (above 10 times the Self-Support Allowance).

Is there a minimum child support order in Delaware?

Yes -- $160/month for one child, $240/month for two or more, regardless of how the Melson Formula calculation comes out, unless the court finds a documented reason to deviate (such as a genuinely unable-to-pay circumstance under the Low-Income Adjustment).
