How California Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Determine each parent's net monthly disposable income (gross income minus taxes, mandatory union dues, mandatory retirement contributions, health premiums, and support paid for other relationships).
  2. Combine both parents' net disposable income (TN) and identify the higher earner's income (HN) and their approximate timeshare percentage (H%).
  3. Compute K: (1+H% if H% is 50% or less, otherwise 2-H%) multiplied by a fraction that depends on TN — 0.165+TN/82,857 up to $2,900, 0.131+TN/42,149 from $2,901-$5,000, a flat 0.250 from $5,001-$10,000, 0.10+1,499/TN from $10,001-$15,000, and 0.12+1,200/TN above $15,000.
  4. Compute the 1-child support amount: CS = K × (HN − H% × TN).
  5. Multiply by the statutory multiplier for the actual number of children (1.6 for 2 children, up to 2.86 for 10).
  6. A positive result means the higher earner pays; a negative result means the lower earner pays the absolute value.

California Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: Parent A (higher earner) net income $5,000/month, Parent B net income $3,000/month, 2 children, Parent A has 20% timeshare.

  1. TN = $8,000/month, HN = $5,000, H% = 20%.
  2. TN falls in the $5,001-$10,000 bracket → fraction = 0.250.
  3. K = (1 + 0.20) × 0.250 = 0.30.
  4. CS (1 child) = 0.30 × ($5,000 − 0.20 × $8,000) = 0.30 × $3,400 = $1,020.
  5. 2-child multiplier ×1.6 = $1,632/month guideline child support paid by Parent A.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in California?

Determine each parent's net monthly disposable income (gross income minus taxes, mandatory union dues, mandatory retirement contributions, health premiums, and support paid for other relationships). Combine both parents' net disposable income (TN) and identify the higher earner's income (HN) and their approximate timeshare percentage (H%). A positive result means the higher earner pays; a negative result means the lower earner pays the absolute value.

What is the California K-factor child support formula?

California's guideline uses the algebraic formula CS = K × (HN − H% × TN), where TN is both parents' combined net disposable income, HN is the higher earner's net income, H% is their approximate percentage of time with the children, and K is a fraction that itself depends on TN (ranging roughly from 0.13 to 0.25+ depending on income level). The result is then multiplied by a statutory multiplier based on the number of children (1.6 for two children, up to 2.86 for ten).

Is California child support based on gross or net income?

Net disposable income — a specific statutory figure, not gross pay or simple take-home pay. It's gross income minus state and federal taxes, mandatory union dues, mandatory retirement contributions, health insurance premiums, and support actually paid for other relationships.

How does timeshare percentage affect California child support?

Timeshare percentage (H%) enters the formula twice: first inside the K fraction itself — K uses (1 + H%) if the higher earner has 50% or less of the time, or (2 − H%) if they have more — and second directly in the base calculation (HN − H% × TN). More time with the higher earner generally reduces the support amount owed, since it changes both the K value and the income figure being multiplied.
