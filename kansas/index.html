How Kansas Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Kansas is unique among the states modeled here: its child support schedule (Appendix II) varies not just by combined income and total number of children, but by each child's AGE -- there are separate 0-5, 6-11, and 12-18 columns, and a separate full schedule table for families with 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6 (or more) children.
  2. Add both parents' gross monthly income to get combined income, then look up the base per-child amount for the 12-18 age bracket in the schedule matching your total number of children.
  3. For each child, adjust that base amount for their own age: multiply by 0.84 for a child age 0-5, by 0.94 for a child age 6-11, or use it unchanged for a child age 12-18 -- this is the guidelines' own stated method for deriving the age columns.
  4. Add up each child's adjusted amount to get the total gross child support obligation.
  5. Each parent's share of that total is their proportion of combined income; the parent with the minority of overnights (under 182.5/year) pays their share to the other parent.
  6. If the parent paying support has 35% or more of the child’s time (a "Parenting Time Adjustment" under Section IV.H.2), the obligation is reduced: 10% at 35-39%, 20% at 40-44%, 30% at 45-49%. At 50% or more, Kansas uses a different shared-residency worksheet not modeled here.
  7. Above $18,000/month combined income, the schedule's top row does not apply automatically -- Kansas's Extended Formula (Section IV.G) computes a presumptive amount from combined income raised to a fixed power, but courts retain discretion at that level.

Kansas Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: Parent A earns $4,000/month gross, Parent B earns $3,000/month gross (combined $7,000). They have two children -- one age 4 (0-5 bracket) and one age 14 (12-18 bracket). The child spends 100 overnights/year with Parent A (27% of the year), so Parent B is the custodial parent and Parent A pays support.

  1. Combined gross monthly income: $4,000 + $3,000 = $7,000.
  2. Two-Child Families schedule at $7,000 combined income, 12-18 base amount: interpolated to about $948/child.
  3. Age 0-5 child's amount: $948 x 0.84 = $796 (rounded).
  4. Age 12-18 child's amount: $948 (unchanged).
  5. Total gross child support obligation: $796 + $948 = $1,744/month.
  6. Parent A's income share: $4,000 / $7,000 = 57.1%.
  7. Parent A's time with the child (27%) is below the 35% Parenting Time Adjustment threshold, so no reduction applies.
  8. Parent A pays: $1,744 x 57.1% = about $997/month.

Why does Kansas ask for each child's age instead of just the number of children?

Kansas's official schedule (Appendix II) publishes a different dollar amount for each child depending on whether they are 0-5, 6-11, or 12-18 years old -- younger children have a lower base amount (84% and 94% of the 12-18 rate, respectively). Most other states use one schedule regardless of a child's specific age.

Does Kansas use gross or net income?

Gross income. Kansas's schedule (Appendix II) is indexed by combined GROSS monthly income -- the schedule itself already builds in an allowance for taxes and other income deductions, so no separate net-income conversion step is required before the lookup.

What happens above Kansas's $18,000/month schedule ceiling?

Kansas's guidelines (Section IV.G) provide an exact Extended Formula for income above the schedule's top row: a fixed coefficient (specific to the number of children) multiplied by combined income raised to the power 0.6386. The amount is presumptive, but courts retain discretion to set a different amount at very high incomes.
