How Mississippi Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Mississippi is a flat-percentage-of-income state: the child support award is a percentage of the noncustodial parent's own adjusted gross income only -- the custodial parent's income is not part of the base calculation.
  2. "Adjusted gross income" starts from gross income from all sources and subtracts federal/state/local taxes, Social Security contributions, mandatory retirement/disability contributions, court-ordered support already owed for other children, and (at the court's discretion) an amount for other children living in the noncustodial parent's home.
  3. Divide the annual adjusted gross income by 12 to get the monthly amount, then multiply by the percentage for the number of children: 14% for one child, 20% for two, 22% for three, 24% for four, 26% for five or more.
  4. When adjusted gross income is above $100,000/year or below $10,000/year, the court must make a written finding on whether the percentage is reasonable in that case -- the percentage itself does not automatically change.
  5. This calculator shows the presumptive guideline amount only; Mississippi courts may deviate for extraordinary expenses, shared parenting arrangements, seasonal income, and other statutory factors.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in Mississippi?

Mississippi is a flat-percentage-of-income state: the child support award is a percentage of the noncustodial parent's own adjusted gross income only -- the custodial parent's income is not part of the base calculation. Does Mississippi use combined parental income like most states?

No. Mississippi Code § 43-19-101 calculates the award as a flat percentage of the NONCUSTODIAL parent's own adjusted gross income only -- the custodial parent's income does not factor into the calculation, unlike income-shares states such as Alabama or Georgia.

What is Mississippi's $100,000 and $10,000 income review?

When the noncustodial parent's adjusted gross income is above $100,000/year or below $10,000/year, the judge must make a written finding on the record as to whether applying the standard percentage is reasonable in that case. This is a required review, not an automatic cap -- the guideline percentage can still apply at any income level.

What counts toward a parent's "adjusted gross income" in Mississippi?

Gross income from virtually all sources (wages, self-employment, investments, benefits, etc.), minus taxes, Social Security, mandatory retirement contributions, and support already ordered for other children (Miss. Code Ann. § 43-19-101(3)).
