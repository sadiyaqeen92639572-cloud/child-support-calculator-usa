How New York Child Support Is Calculated

  1. Combine both parents' income to get combined parental income.
  2. Apply the statutory percentage based on number of children (17% for 1 child, 25% for 2, 29% for 3, 31% for 4, 35% for 5 or more) to combined income up to the cap ($193,000/year, effective 2026-03-01).
  3. The parent with fewer overnights (even close to 50/50) pays their pro-rata share of that amount — New York does not use a shared-parenting offset formula.
  4. Health insurance, work/education-related childcare, and unreimbursed medical costs are mandatory add-ons split pro rata by income share, on top of the basic obligation.
  5. Above the income cap, courts apply 10 statutory factors rather than the automatic percentage.

New York Child Support Worksheet Walkthrough

Example: combined parental income of $6,000/month, 2 children, Parent A earns $4,000/mo (67% share) and Parent B earns $2,000/mo (33% share) and has fewer overnights.

  1. Combined income: $6,000/month (below the $16,083/month cap).
  2. 2 children → statutory percentage: 25%.
  3. $6,000 × 0.25 = $1,500/month basic obligation.
  4. Parent A's share (67%) of $1,500 = $1,000/month guideline child support paid by Parent A.

FAQ

How is child support calculated in New York?

Combine both parents' income to get combined parental income. Apply the statutory percentage based on number of children (17% for 1 child, 25% for 2, 29% for 3, 31% for 4, 35% for 5 or more) to combined income up to the cap ($193,000/year, effective 2026-03-01). The parent with fewer overnights (even close to 50/50) pays their pro-rata share of that amount — New York does not use a shared-parenting offset formula. Health insurance, work/education-related childcare, and unreimbursed medical costs are mandatory add-ons split pro rata by income share, on top of the basic obligation. Above the income cap, courts apply 10 statutory factors rather than the automatic percentage.

Does New York reduce child support for shared/50-50 custody?

No. New York's CSSA formula does not include a shared-parenting or overnight-based offset like income-shares states use. Even in a near-50/50 schedule, whichever parent has fewer overnights (as designated by the court) still pays their full pro-rata share of the statutory percentage — there's no built-in credit for shared time, though a court retains discretion to deviate.

What counts as income under the New York CSSA?

Combined parental income for CSSA purposes starts from gross income and is adjusted for statutory items such as FICA/Medicare taxes, NYC or Yonkers income tax if applicable, and maintenance (spousal support) actually paid to the other parent. It is not simply take-home pay.

What happens if combined income is above $193,000/year?

Above the $193,000/year combined income cap (effective 2026-03-01), courts stop applying the automatic statutory percentage. Instead, they weigh 10 factors set out in the CSSA — including the standard of living the child would have had, the non-monied parent's needs, and the parties' financial resources — to decide support on the portion above the cap.
